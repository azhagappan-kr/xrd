How does a Powder XRD Instrument Work? â–¼

A powder X-ray diffractometer (PXRD) operates in the following sequence:

  • X-ray generation: A high-voltage current excites a metal anode (e.g., Cu) to emit characteristic X-rays.
  • Beam conditioning: Slits and monochromators select and collimate the beam to the desired wavelength and width.
  • Sample interaction: The beam strikes the powdered sample mounted on a flat holder. In a powder, crystallites are randomly oriented, so all possible lattice planes simultaneously satisfy Bragg's condition at the appropriate angles.
  • Detection: A detector (e.g., scintillation counter or position-sensitive detector) scans a range of 2Î¸ angles and records the intensity of diffracted X-rays.
  • Diffractogram: The output is an intensity vs. 2Î¸ plot â€” each peak corresponding to a specific set of lattice planes (hkl) in the material.

The peak positions, intensities, and widths carry information about crystal structure, phase composition, crystallite size, and lattice strain.

Sample Preparation

Proper preparation ensures high-quality, reproducible data.

Grinding and Particle Size â–¼

Samples should be ground to a fine powder (ideally <10 Âµm) to ensure random crystallite orientation and reduce preferred orientation artefacts.

  • Use an agate mortar and pestle for soft/brittle materials.
  • Use a vibratory ball mill for harder materials; avoid over-milling which can induce amorphisation.
  • Minimum recommended quantity: ~50 mg (more is better for flat-plate geometry).
Sample Mounting â–¼

The sample must be loaded into the holder so that the surface is flat and flush with the reference surface.

  • Back-loading: Preferred method for minimising preferred orientation. Pack powder from the back of the holder.
  • Front-loading: Simple, but can introduce preferred orientation in platy materials (clays, graphite).
  • Zero-background holders: Recommended for small sample quantities to eliminate substrate reflections.
  • Avoid pressing the powder too firmly â€” this can cause preferred orientation.
Special Sample Types â–¼
  • Thin films: Use grazing incidence XRD (GIXRD) geometry; inform the facility in advance.
  • Air-sensitive samples: Can be measured under an inert atmosphere using an appropriate dome/cover; contact the facility before your session.
  • Hygroscopic samples: Prepare immediately before measurement; avoid moisture absorption during transfer.
  • Biological / pharmaceutical samples: Notify staff for guidance on scan parameters and containment requirements.

Safety Guidelines

Radiation safety and laboratory best practices.

Radiation Safety â–¼

X-ray diffractometers are enclosed radiation sources. Modern instruments have interlocked enclosures that prevent beam exposure during operation. Always observe the following:

  • Never defeat or override safety interlocks.
  • Do not attempt to open the instrument enclosure while the X-ray shutter is open.
  • Personal dosimetry badges must be worn by all regular users as required by institutional radiation safety policy.
  • Follow all institutional Radiation Safety Officer (RSO) instructions and report any suspected exposure immediately.
Chemical and Sample Handling Safety â–¼
  • Wear appropriate PPE (gloves, lab coat, safety glasses) when handling samples.
  • Toxic, carcinogenic, or reactive samples require prior written approval and may need special containment.
  • Radioactive samples are strictly prohibited unless specifically authorised by the RSO and facility manager.
  • Label all sample containers clearly with sample ID and user name.
  • Clean up any spilled sample powder immediately to prevent contamination of the instrument.
General Lab Rules â–¼
  • No food or drink in the instrument room.
  • Log all instrument use in the facility log book.
  • Report any instrument malfunction or unusual behaviour to the facility manager immediately; do not attempt self-repair.
  • All users must complete the facility induction/training session before independent access is granted.
